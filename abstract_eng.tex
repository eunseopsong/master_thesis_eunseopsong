\begin{center}
\Large\textbf{Hybrid Vision-Based Geometric Planning and Force-Aware Imitation Learning for Adaptive Robotic Surface Finishing}
\end{center}

Robotic surface finishing requires both geometry-dependent global coverage and contact-dependent local interaction. Geometry-based planning can provide interpretable surface paths and explicit coverage constraints, but repeated changes in workpiece pose, robot initial configuration, and requested region of interest require repeated perception, registration, inverse kinematics, and motion planning. End-to-end visuomotor imitation learning can generate actions with low online inference latency, but vision-only policies do not directly observe contact pressure, slip, jamming, or weak contact, and they do not by themselves guarantee deterministic full-surface coverage.

This thesis proposes a hybrid framework that learns where interaction-dependent adaptation is required and uses geometric planning where deterministic coverage can be explicitly specified. The local component is an ROI-mask-conditioned force-aware imitation-learning policy. The policy receives visual observation, an externally generated region-of-interest mask, robot proprioception, and recent six-axis force/torque history, and generates task-conditioned pose--force action chunks. The learned reference is executed through a low-level admittance controller, which converts the error between measured wrench and desired force reference into compliant motion correction. The global component reconstructs or registers the workpiece surface, generates tool-footprint-aware coverage paths, assigns tool orientation from local surface normals, and executes the resulting trajectory with the same contact-aware execution layer.

Two workpieces are used to evaluate the framework. The first is an automotive headlight, where an ArUco marker is used only as a low-cost command interface that emulates an external defect-map request. The marker ID is converted outside the policy into a spatial ROI mask; the policy observes the mask rather than the marker ID. The local polishing phase is followed by vision-based full-coverage polishing of the headlight surface. The second workpiece is an aluminum machining specimen for pose-randomized deburring or deburring-like edge-following contact execution. This benchmark compares a classical perception--registration--planning pipeline with a vision-conditioned IL policy under variations in workpiece pose, robot initial configuration, target feature, and approach direction.

The proposed experiments evaluate ROI-mask-conditioned local processing, local-to-global hybrid polishing feasibility, online latency and cycle-time decomposition, and the role of force observation, learned force references, and admittance execution. Quantitative values for task success, force error, cycle time, and surface-quality metrics are not reported in this draft and remain \todoexp{to be filled after real-robot experiments and inspection measurements}.
