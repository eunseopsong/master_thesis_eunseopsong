\begin{center}
\Large\textbf{FAIRE: Force-Aware Imitation with Reactive Execution for Robotic Polishing}
\end{center}

Robotic polishing requires more than reaching a target region or completing a geometric path. Consistent surface finishing depends on reproducing continuous contact behavior, including the applied force trajectory, sliding velocity, and alignment between the polishing tool and the workpiece surface. A vision-only imitation policy can reproduce demonstrated motion, but it does not directly observe contact. Conversely, a force-aware policy must do more than recognize contact: it must convert the demonstrated interaction into a reference that can be executed safely and compliantly on a robot.

This thesis presents FAIRE, a precision-oriented framework for force-aware imitation with reactive execution in robotic polishing. FAIRE begins with a handheld teaching device equipped with a six-axis force/torque sensor and tracked in a virtual-reality coordinate system. An explicit VR-to-robot calibration transfers synchronized position, orientation, wrench, and image demonstrations into the robot workspace. A gated recurrent unit encodes a window of wrench measurements to represent contact evolution. Fused visual, proprioceptive, and force-history features are then used to predict short-horizon position and orientation references together with a desired force trajectory.

The learned desired force is a controller-level reference rather than a direct actuator command. During execution, an admittance controller converts the error between measured and desired force into a compliant Cartesian position correction. Two bounded residual reinforcement-learning policies operate as precision-correction layers above this nominal execution: one scales the sliding velocity according to the contact state, and the other corrects the tool orientation to improve alignment between the tool-center-point normal and the estimated surface normal. The imitation policy and admittance controller remain responsible for task-level execution.

The evaluation is organized around three questions: whether a time-varying motion--force action improves contact reproduction, which force-history representation best tracks demonstrated force trajectories, and whether residual velocity and orientation corrections improve polishing precision. The protocol measures normal-force error, force-profile correlation, overshoot, contact loss, normal-alignment error, surface-quality improvement, spatial consistency, and task time on curved headlight surfaces. Because the source FAIRE manuscript does not yet contain completed measurements, quantitative conclusions and unverified implementation parameters are explicitly marked as planned or TBD rather than inferred.
