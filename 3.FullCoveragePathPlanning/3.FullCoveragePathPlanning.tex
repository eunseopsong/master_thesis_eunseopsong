\chapter{FAIRE Overview and Problem Formulation}
\label{chap:faire_overview}

\section{System Objective}
FAIRE aims to reproduce the continuous contact behavior demonstrated during robotic polishing. Let the robot observation at time \(t\) contain an RGB image \(I_t\), a proprioceptive and pose state \(s_t\), and the measured six-axis wrench
\begin{equation}
  w_t=[F_x,F_y,F_z,\tau_x,\tau_y,\tau_z]_t.
  \label{eq:wrench}
\end{equation}
The image localizes the task region, the pose state describes the robot configuration and tool motion, and the wrench describes interaction with the workpiece.

The task output is a short horizon of reference position, reference orientation, and desired contact force:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{A}_t =
  \left\{
    x^{\mathrm{ref}}_{t:t+H},
    R^{\mathrm{ref}}_{t:t+H},
    F^{\mathrm{des}}_{t:t+H}
  \right\}.
  \label{eq:faire_action}
\end{equation}
Here \(H\) is the prediction horizon. The desired force is a trajectory to be tracked by a compliant controller; it is not a direct joint-torque or actuator-level command.

The framework contains three stages. First, the teaching stage maps handheld motion into the robot base frame through an explicit calibration chain and stores synchronized image, pose, and wrench measurements. Second, the learning stage encodes the current visual and pose context together with a wrench-history window. Third, the execution stage uses admittance control to realize the desired force and residual policies to correct velocity and orientation.

\section{Temporal Contact Representation}
Polishing contact is history dependent. The same instantaneous normal force can occur during initial contact, stable sliding, or recovery from excessive load. FAIRE therefore uses the wrench window
\begin{equation}
  w_{t-K:t}=\{w_{t-K},\ldots,w_t\},
  \label{eq:wrench_history}
\end{equation}
where \(K+1\) is the history length. The recurrent representation is
\begin{equation}
  z_t^F=\mathrm{GRU}(w_{t-K:t}).
  \label{eq:force_gru}
\end{equation}
Separate visual and pose encoders produce \(z_t^I=f_I(I_t)\) and \(z_t^P=f_P(s_t)\). Their fused latent state,
\begin{equation}
  z_t=\mathrm{Fusion}(z_t^I,z_t^P,z_t^F),
  \label{eq:fusion}
\end{equation}
conditions the motion--force policy. Separation is important to the planned ablation: unified and split encoders, current and historical force inputs, and recurrent alternatives can be compared while holding the action and controller interfaces fixed.

\section{Nominal and Residual Responsibilities}
The nominal policy \(\pi_\theta\) produces the task-level reference,
\begin{equation}
  \pi_\theta(z_t)\mapsto\mathcal{A}_t.
  \label{eq:motion_force_policy}
\end{equation}
The admittance controller maps force error into the compliant position adjustment needed to realize this reference. Residual policies then make bounded precision corrections:
\begin{align}
  v_t^{\mathrm{cmd}} &= \alpha_t v_t^{\mathrm{ref}},\\
  R_t^{\mathrm{cmd}} &= R_t^{\mathrm{ref}}\Delta R_t^{\mathrm{RL}}.
  \label{eq:residual_commands_overview}
\end{align}
The velocity scale \(\alpha_t\) and residual rotation \(\Delta R_t^{\mathrm{RL}}\) are restricted by safety and smoothness limits. They do not generate a new path, target region, or force trajectory.

\section{Design Principles}
Four principles constrain the framework.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Synchronized multimodal teaching:} pose, orientation, image, and wrench must share a common time base and coordinate convention.
  \item \textbf{Explicit execution semantics:} the predicted desired force must be realized through a specified controller rather than described ambiguously as a robot action.
  \item \textbf{Temporal contact context:} the force representation must distinguish contact phases that are not identifiable from a single sample.
  \item \textbf{Bounded correction:} RL is limited to precision adjustments; nominal task execution remains interpretable through the demonstrated motion--force reference.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Assumptions and Safety Boundary}
The target polishing region is specified before demonstration and evaluation. The framework assumes that the task remains within the demonstrated visual, geometric, and contact distribution and that the robot provides a Cartesian command interface compatible with admittance correction. Wrench bias compensation, frame transformation, filtering, action limits, force limits, velocity limits, workspace limits, and emergency-stop logic are required implementation elements.
