\section{Motivation}
The headlight demonstration combines two task types. A local ROI requires condition-dependent contact behavior: approach, contact establishment, short traversal, force modulation, and retraction. The remaining visible surface requires deterministic full-coverage polishing. The proposed workflow therefore uses force-aware IL for the requested local region and geometry-based planning for the global surface.

Several recent systems are close to this learning--geometry integration. Visual-guided Diffusion Policy with Mesh-DMP combines learned action generation with an explicit surface-constrained motion representation for freeform polishing~\cite{ke2025visualguided}. RoboGrind integrates scanning, defect identification, robot program generation, and force-controlled surface treatment~\cite{alt2024robogrind}. Ergodic surface finishing uses tool imprints, desired spatial distributions, and learned motion preferences for surface treatment~\cite{schneyer2026ergodic}. SRDP studies stage-aware robotic polishing with feed-speed and normal-force generation under roughness-oriented constraints~\cite{ke2026srdp}. Learning-augmented robotic automation further frames learned controllers inside conventional industrial workflows~\cite{kim2026learningaugmented}. The contribution here is therefore limited to the planned evaluation of ROI-mask-conditioned local force-aware processing, explicit global coverage, and latency/ablation comparisons on the selected workpieces.

\section{ArUco-to-ROI-Mask Interface}
ArUco markers are used as a low-cost command interface that emulates an external defect-request or defect-map system~\cite{garrido2014aruco}. The marker does not sense a defect, is not treated as defect evidence, and is not polished away. The mapping is:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item detect the ArUco marker in a command camera or a region separated from the policy image;
    \item decode the marker ID outside the policy;
    \item retrieve predefined ROI information or a synthetic defect-map request;
    \item generate a spatial ROI mask in the headlight or local-camera coordinate frame;
    \item provide the ROI mask, not the marker ID, to the IL policy.
\end{enumerate}
If the marker appears in the RGB image used by the policy, the marker region must be cropped, masked out, or captured by a separate command camera. This prevents the policy from learning a shortcut from marker pattern to trajectory. The demonstration should not be structured as a lookup table from three or four IDs to three or four trajectories.

\section{ROI Mask Construction}
The ROI mask can be binary or soft:
\begin{equation}
M_{\mathrm{ROI}}:\Pi(\mathcal{S}_H)\rightarrow[0,1],
\end{equation}
where $\Pi(\mathcal{S}_H)$ denotes the image or surface projection of the headlight surface. Training masks should vary in center location, size, shape, orientation, and curvature region. Shapes may include circular, elliptical, elongated, and irregular regions. The split should include seen mask shape with unseen location, seen location range with unseen orientation, unseen size within the training range, held-out combinations of location/size/orientation, and optionally small workpiece-pose perturbations.

\section{Local Force-Aware IL Phase}
The local policy receives the observation in Eq.~\eqref{eq:il_observation}. It generates an action chunk for:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item approach to a safe pre-contact pose;
    \item contact establishment with bounded force overshoot;
    \item local traversal over the requested ROI;
    \item force modulation or force-reference tracking;
    \item retract to a transition pose for global polishing.
\end{enumerate}
The local success criteria are defined by footprint coverage of the ROI, spillover outside the ROI, force stability, contact-loss count, and completion time. Actual defect removal, transparency recovery, or gloss restoration is evaluated only after standardized physical defects and inspection measurements are implemented.

\section{Global Full-Coverage Phase}
After local IL inference and execution, the system executes a global path:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item acquire or update the headlight surface reconstruction;
    \item register the surface to the robot base frame;
    \item process the mesh or point cloud and estimate normals;
    \item generate a tool-footprint-aware coverage path;
    \item assign local surface-normal-based tool orientations;
    \item execute the trajectory with force/admittance control.
\end{enumerate}
The local ROI phase and global phase can overlap spatially if the full-coverage process intentionally blends the local region into the rest of the surface. Any overlap is reported as part of the coverage and spillover metrics rather than treated as unmeasured physical-defect removal.

\section{Transition, Safety, and Failure Handling}
The transition from local to global processing requires a safe retract pose, a validated current robot state, and either a known workpiece transform or a fresh registration. The safety supervisor monitors workspace limits, collision margin, normal force, tangential force, tool moment, velocity, and spindle state. Failure handling includes controlled retraction, request for re-registration, rejection of unreachable ROI requests, and operator intervention for out-of-distribution masks.

\section{Headlight Method Variants}
The headlight comparison includes four minimum variants:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{H1 Geometry-based full coverage only:} geometry-based global coverage without a local ROI phase.
    \item \textbf{H2 Geometry-derived local + global hybrid:} ROI-mask-derived raster or contour local path with fixed force or conventional force control, followed by global coverage.
    \item \textbf{H3 Pose-only IL + global hybrid:} RGB/mask/state policy that outputs pose trajectory and uses fixed-force or conventional force control, followed by global coverage.
    \item \textbf{H4 Proposed force-aware IL:} RGB/mask/state/F/T-history policy that outputs pose plus verified desired force channels and executes with admittance, followed by global coverage.
\end{enumerate}
H2 is essential because it tests whether a simple local geometry-derived path generated from the same ROI mask is sufficient.

\section{Future Inspection Interface}
The ArUco-based command interface is a temporary substitute for a real defect-inspection output. Future work should replace it with a high-resolution inspection system that produces a defect map or condition mask. At that stage, additional metrics such as defect-area reduction, gloss or transparency restoration, required number of passes, over-polishing area, and total cycle time to target quality can be reported. Until then, the thesis uses the terms ROI-mask-conditioned local processing or defect-map-conditioned local processing and avoids terminology that implies autonomous defect inspection and repair.
