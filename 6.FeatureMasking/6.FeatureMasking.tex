\chapter{Residual Reinforcement Learning for Precision Correction}
\label{chap:residual_rl}

\section{Role of the Residual Layer}
The nominal motion--force policy and admittance controller define a complete polishing execution path. Residual RL is added only to correct variables that strongly affect polishing precision under local contact and curvature variation. This decomposition narrows the learning problem and keeps task progress anchored to the demonstration.

The residual layer contains two policies. The velocity policy adjusts progress along the reference stroke, and the orientation policy adjusts alignment between the tool and surface normals. Their actions are bounded and regularized for smoothness. Neither policy selects the target region, generates the nominal path, nor replaces the desired force trajectory.

\section{Adaptive Sliding-Velocity Policy}
Let
\begin{equation}
  e_F(t)=F_t^{\mathrm{meas}}-F_t^{\mathrm{des}}
  \label{eq:force_error}
\end{equation}
denote the force-tracking error. The velocity policy observes this error, recent wrench history, and robot state:
\begin{equation}
  \alpha_t=\pi_v(e_F(t),w_{t-K:t},s_t).
  \label{eq:velocity_residual}
\end{equation}
The commanded tangential velocity is
\begin{equation}
  v_t^{\mathrm{cmd}}=\alpha_t v_t^{\mathrm{ref}},
  \label{eq:velocity_command}
\end{equation}
where \(\alpha_t\) is constrained to a safe interval. Reducing progress can give the admittance loop time to recover from a force deviation, while increasing progress can reduce excessive dwell when contact is stable. Because speed also affects material-removal rate, the policy objective must balance force tracking, finish consistency, execution time, smoothness, and safety.

A generic planned reward decomposition is
\begin{equation}
  r_t^v =
  -c_F(e_F)
  -c_{\Delta\alpha}(\alpha_t-\alpha_{t-1})
  -c_{\mathrm{safety}}
  +r_{\mathrm{progress}},
  \label{eq:velocity_reward}
\end{equation}
where each term must be defined and scaled from measured quantities. Equation~\eqref{eq:velocity_reward} records the intended objective categories, not finalized reward weights.

\section{Tool-Normal Alignment Policy}
Let \(n_t^{\mathrm{tcp}}\) be the tool-center-point normal and \(n_t^{\mathrm{surf}}\) the estimated local surface normal. Their angular discrepancy is
\begin{equation}
  e_n(t)=
  \cos^{-1}\!\left(
  \operatorname{clip}\left(
  (n_t^{\mathrm{tcp}})^\top n_t^{\mathrm{surf}},-1,1
  \right)\right).
  \label{eq:normal_alignment_error}
\end{equation}
The orientation policy produces a bounded residual rotation:
\begin{align}
  \Delta R_t^{\mathrm{RL}}
  &=\pi_R(n_t^{\mathrm{tcp}},n_t^{\mathrm{surf}},w_{t-K:t},s_t),\\
  R_t^{\mathrm{cmd}}
  &=R_t^{\mathrm{ref}}\Delta R_t^{\mathrm{RL}}.
  \label{eq:orientation_command}
\end{align}
The multiplication order in Eq.~\eqref{eq:orientation_command} defines the residual in the local reference frame. A different implemented convention must be documented explicitly.

A planned orientation reward can combine normal-alignment error, force tracking, rotation magnitude, temporal smoothness, and safety:
\begin{equation}
  r_t^R =
  -c_n(e_n)
  -c_F(e_F)
  -c_R(\Delta R_t^{\mathrm{RL}})
  -c_{\Delta R}
  -c_{\mathrm{safety}}.
  \label{eq:orientation_reward}
\end{equation}

\section{Training Interface}
The source FAIRE manuscript does not name the RL algorithms or state whether the policies are trained in simulation, from logged data, on the physical robot, or through a staged combination. Consequently, this thesis does not infer an algorithm. The final report must specify the Markov state, action parameterization, observation normalization, algorithm, network size, discount, optimizer, replay or rollout scheme, environment randomization, termination conditions, and number of training steps.

Safe training should begin with a verified nominal controller and conservative action bounds. Physical-robot exploration, if used, requires force, velocity, orientation, workspace, and cumulative-load limits plus an independent stop mechanism. A residual action must be clipped before command composition, not only discouraged by reward.

\section{Policy Interaction}
The two residual policies may interact because velocity affects force tracking and orientation affects contact distribution. The planned ablation therefore evaluates no residual policy, velocity only, orientation only, and both policies. All four variants retain the same imitation representation, force-trajectory output, and admittance controller so that performance changes can be attributed to the correction layer.

Joint execution also requires a specified command order. The conceptual FAIRE order computes the admittance position correction from force error, scales tangential reference velocity, applies the bounded orientation residual, and then enforces final safety limits. Controller implementation, update rates, and arbitration rules remain \todoexp{TBD}.

\section{Expected Failure Modes}
Potential failures include velocity oscillation, residual saturation, inaccurate surface normals, overcorrection on high curvature, conflict between force and orientation objectives, and distribution shift outside the demonstrations or RL training environment. Evaluation should report safety interventions and saturation frequency alongside average performance. A gain in mean polishing quality is insufficient if it is accompanied by rare excessive-force events.
