\section{Robotic Surface Finishing}
Robotic polishing, grinding, sanding, and deburring combine geometric path generation with physical interaction. RoboGrind is a close system-level reference because it combines 3-D scanning, defect identification, robot program generation, and force-controlled surface treatment in an integrated workflow~\cite{alt2024robogrind}. This thesis therefore does not treat the combination of inspection, geometric planning, and force-controlled execution as new by itself.

Geometry-based coverage planning remains the standard mechanism when a surface, tool footprint, boundary, and feasibility model are available. Freeform-surface planning studies have considered segmentation, tool geometry, contact point selection, curvature, and manipulator/task constraints~\cite{schneyer2023segmentation,mcgovern2024coverage}. Ergodic surface finishing further uses a desired spatial distribution, tool imprint, and learned motion preferences to guide online surface treatment~\cite{schneyer2026ergodic}. These works support the global stage of this thesis: deterministic coverage is assigned to a geometric planner rather than to an end-to-end policy.

Learning-based surface motion has also entered polishing and surface-constrained manipulation. Visual-guided Diffusion Policy with Mesh-DMP integrates learned action generation with explicit surface-constrained motion embedding for robotic freeform polishing~\cite{ke2025visualguided}. Surface Constraint Policy uses visual observations and learned surface constraints to generate dynamically feasible surface-constrained skills~\cite{ke2026surfaceconstraint}. Stage-Aware and Roughness-Constrained Diffusion Policy targets multi-stage robotic polishing and generates process-related feed speed and normal contact force under roughness-oriented constraints~\cite{ke2026srdp}. These studies overlap strongly with surface-aware action generation and process-parameter learning; the distinction in this thesis is the explicit allocation of local ROI-conditioned interaction to force-aware IL and global full-coverage execution to a geometry planner.

Deburring is related but not identical to polishing. A rigid cutter, abrasive point, or compliant finishing tool follows an edge, hole, or chamfer while maintaining acceptable tool load and avoiding collision. Learning-guided force-feedback MPC for robotic deburring combines a diffusion motion prior, force-feedback MPC, and obstacle avoidance~\cite{wojciechowski2026deburring}. This thesis cites that work as a deburring and learned-warm-start reference, not as evidence for validated material-removal quality in the present aluminum experiment. If the aluminum specimen is smooth or burr geometry is not measured, the task is reported as force-controlled edge-following or deburring-like contact execution.

\section{3-D Reconstruction and Coverage Path Planning}
Coverage planning on a three-dimensional surface seeks a path whose swept tool footprint covers the target region. The important footprint is the effective contact footprint of the polishing or deburring tool, not the tool center point. Freeform-surface planners and system studies motivate the use of surface normals, tool-contact modeling, adaptive spacing, segmentation, reachability checks, and manipulator constraints~\cite{tafuro2024autonomous,schneyer2023segmentation,mcgovern2024coverage,alt2024robogrind}.

The limitation considered here is not task impossibility. Rather, when the same rigid task must be repeated under many workpiece poses, robot initial configurations, or requested ROIs, the perception--registration--IK--planning loop is repeatedly invoked. The proposed framework keeps geometry-based planning as the deterministic coverage mechanism and as a strong classical baseline, while evaluating whether learned policies can amortize online computation for repeated local or pose-conditioned queries.

\section{Force, Impedance, and Admittance Control}
Force and compliance control are standard tools for contact-rich robotic manipulation~\cite{hogan1985impedance,keemink2018admittance,villani2008forcecontrol}. In admittance control, measured force error is mapped into motion correction. This is appropriate for industrial position-controlled manipulators that accept pose or velocity commands. The high-level policy should therefore be distinguished from the low-level controller: the policy proposes task-conditioned pose and force references, while the controller stabilizes contact and enforces the reference within safety limits.

\section{Visuomotor Imitation Learning and Generative Policies}
Visuomotor imitation learning maps sensory observations to robot actions from demonstrations. Action chunking predicts a short future sequence rather than a single immediate command; ACT introduced this idea with transformer policies for fine-grained manipulation~\cite{zhao2023act}. Diffusion Policy models multimodal action distributions through conditional action diffusion and receding-horizon execution~\cite{chi2025diffusionpolicy}. Flow-matching policies are relevant when the implemented backbone uses flow-based generation: Riemannian Flow Matching Policy studies manifold-aware robot motion learning with fast inference~\cite{braun2024riemannianflow}.

These references are used to position the policy-learning mechanism, not to rename the implemented method. If the final implementation uses Flow Matching, Chapter~\ref{chap:force_il} describes it as a conditional flow-matching action-chunk policy. ACT and Diffusion Policy remain related-work references for action chunks and generative action distributions.

\section{Force-Aware and Compliance-Aware Imitation Learning}
Recent work has added force and tactile information to imitation learning for contact-rich manipulation. Admittance Visuomotor Policy Learning is the most direct structural overlap: it uses vision, proprioception, and force observation to generate action trajectories and desired contact forces, then executes them with an admittance controller~\cite{zhou2024admitdiff}. ForceMimic emphasizes force-motion capture, wrench-position parameter prediction, and hybrid force-position execution~\cite{liu2025forcemimic}. FAWAM encodes historical six-axis force/torque signals, jointly predicts future actions and wrenches, and uses force residual correction during execution~\cite{he2026fawam}. Force Policy separates a global vision policy from a high-frequency local force policy in an interaction frame for hybrid force-position control~\cite{fang2026forcepolicy}. FoAR uses high-frequency F/T input with visual observations for reactive multimodal fusion~\cite{he2025foar}.

Other compliance-aware policy studies are relevant to the controller boundary. Adaptive Compliance Policy learns approximate compliance profiles for diffusion-guided control~\cite{hou2025adaptivecompliance}; Reactive Diffusion Policy combines a slow visual policy with a fast tactile/force loop~\cite{xue2025reactivediffusion}; Flow with the Force Field studies compliant flow-matching policies from force and demonstration-guided simulation data~\cite{li2026flowforce}; and Force Generative Imitation Learning estimates force commands from position trajectories through a control-oriented formulation~\cite{sato2026forcegenerative}. These works mean that F/T observation, F/T history, pose-plus-force output, desired force references, admittance execution, high-frequency force correction, and polishing force generation are not treated as standalone novelty claims in this thesis.

\section{Mask-Conditioned Policies}
Mask-conditioned policies provide task-relevant spatial information explicitly. Transferring Foundation Models for Generalizable Robotic Manipulation uses raw RGB and segmentation masks in a two-stream imitation-learning policy, making the semantic mask an explicit task condition~\cite{yang2025foundation}. VIOLA uses object proposal priors to form object-centric representations and attend to task-relevant visual factors~\cite{zhu2023viola}. Mask2Act uses future object masks as a latent plan for action-free video pre-training~\cite{zhang2025mask2act}. GROOT uses object-centric 3-D representations and segmentation correspondence for generalizable manipulation~\cite{zhu2023groot}.

The proposed ROI mask is therefore not presented as a new visual representation by itself. It is used as an explicit and transferable task-specification interface for local force-aware surface processing. In the headlight setup, the mask is generated from an external command or inspection request. The initial ArUco implementation is only a low-cost command interface~\cite{garrido2014aruco}; the marker ID is not a policy observation and is not treated as visual evidence of a physical defect.

\section{Learned and Amortized Motion Planning}
Learning-based motion planning can be interpreted as amortizing repeated planning queries. Motion Planning Networks learn fast motion-plan predictions and can be combined with classical planning for feasibility repair~\cite{qureshi2019mpnet}. MPC-MPNet combines learned proposals with model-predictive refinement under kinodynamic constraints~\cite{li2021mpcmpnet}. Motion Planning Diffusion learns trajectory-distribution priors with diffusion models and adapts them with motion-planning costs~\cite{carvalho2025motionplanningdiffusion}. Point-cloud-conditioned flow matching has also been studied as a warm start for optimization-based manipulator motion planning~\cite{tian2025flowwarmstart}.

This perspective motivates RQ3. The aluminum experiment does not claim that IL generally replaces classical planning. Classical planners retain advantages in collision checking, explicit constraints, and feasibility repair. The planned comparison measures latency, latency variance, cycle time, contact stability, and task success under a shared execution layer.

The comparison shows that most individual components of the proposed workflow already exist in the literature. The thesis therefore positions its contribution as a task-specific integration and evaluation: an external ROI interface for local contact-rich surface processing, force-aware local pose--force generation, deterministic full-surface coverage, and a latency/cycle-time benchmark against a classical perception--registration--planning pipeline. This is not framed as an absolute novelty claim.

\section{Research Gap}
The reviewed literature motivates the thesis around a specific intersection. Surface-finishing systems already integrate perception, planning, and force control; force-aware IL already predicts motion and force-related references; mask-conditioned manipulation already uses spatial task conditions; and learned planners already amortize repeated planning queries. The planned contribution is to evaluate a local-to-global surface-finishing workflow in which interaction-dependent local processing and deterministic global coverage are assigned to different solvers, and to test the resulting force-aware ablations and planning-latency trade-offs on complementary headlight and aluminum workpieces.
