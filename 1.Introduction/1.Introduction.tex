\chapter{Introduction}
\section{Background and Motivation}
Robotic surface finishing, including polishing, sanding, deburring, grinding, and buffing, is a physically demanding and quality-sensitive operation. Integrated systems such as RoboGrind show that surface scanning, defect identification, program generation, and force-controlled execution are all relevant to practical robotic surface treatment~\cite{alt2024robogrind}. A successful finishing process depends on the nominal tool path, surface normal, tool footprint, contact force, sliding direction, dwell time, abrasive state, and robot feasibility. These factors are coupled: a path that covers a mathematical surface does not necessarily cover the surface with the physical tool, and a geometrically valid pose may still produce unstable or insufficient contact.

Geometry-based planning is therefore indispensable for many finishing tasks. Given a CAD model or a reconstructed surface, a planner can compute coverage paths, surface-normal tool orientations, boundary offsets, and robot feasibility constraints. Recent freeform-surface coverage planners explicitly consider segmentation, contact points, and manipulator constraints~\cite{schneyer2023segmentation,mcgovern2024coverage}; ergodic surface-finishing methods additionally connect tool imprints and learned motion preferences to spatial coverage objectives~\cite{schneyer2026ergodic}. Such methods are strong when the target region is known and the main objective is deterministic coverage. This thesis does not treat geometry-based planning as a weak baseline or as an approach that cannot perform the task.

At the same time, surface-finishing cells increasingly face repeated variation in workpiece pose, requested region of interest (ROI), robot initial configuration, and local contact condition. In such settings, a classical pipeline must repeatedly perform perception, reconstruction or registration, target transformation, inverse kinematics, collision-aware motion planning, trajectory post-processing, and recovery. Imitation learning (IL) offers a complementary mechanism: demonstrations and planning solutions can be compressed into a policy that generates task-conditioned action chunks with low online inference latency. This is an amortized motion-generation hypothesis, not a claim that geometric planning is infeasible.

Contact-rich finishing also exposes a limitation of vision-only policies. Images and depth observations are useful for free-space approach and task semantics, but they do not directly measure contact pressure, weak contact, slip, tool jamming, or overload. Force-conditioned visuomotor policies already combine vision, proprioception, force observation, desired force references, and compliant execution for contact-rich manipulation~\cite{zhou2024admitdiff,liu2025forcemimic}. Recent policies also study global/local force control, high-frequency force/torque feedback, and force-aware reactive fusion~\cite{fang2026forcepolicy,he2025foar}. A low-level admittance controller then converts force-reference error into compliant motion correction, following established force and compliance-control principles~\cite{hogan1985impedance,keemink2018admittance,villani2008forcecontrol}.

Recent studies have already demonstrated force-conditioned visuomotor policies, learned force references, compliant execution, mask-conditioned manipulation policies, surface-constrained imitation learning, robotic-polishing generative policies, and hybrid learning--geometry systems~\cite{zhou2024admitdiff,yang2025foundation,ke2025visualguided,ke2026surfaceconstraint,ke2026srdp,kim2026learningaugmented}. Therefore, this thesis does not claim novelty from these individual components in isolation. Its focus is narrower: an external defect request is represented as a spatial ROI mask, interaction-dependent local processing is assigned to a force-aware policy, deterministic full-surface coverage is assigned to a geometry planner, and the resulting workflow is evaluated with force-observation/action ablations and a planning-latency benchmark.

This thesis proposes a hybrid framework with a simple allocation principle: learn where interaction-dependent adaptation is required, and use geometric planning where deterministic coverage can be explicitly specified. The local component is an ROI-mask-conditioned force-aware IL policy that generates pose--force action chunks for contact-sensitive local processing. The global component is a vision- or reconstruction-based geometry planner that generates tool-footprint-aware full-coverage trajectories. Both components share a common execution layer based on force/torque sensing and admittance control.

\section{Research Questions}
The thesis is organized around four research questions.

\textbf{RQ1: ROI-mask-conditioned local processing.} Can a force-aware imitation-learning policy generate task-appropriate local pose--force trajectories for spatially varying ROI masks on a curved workpiece? For the headlight task, the policy should respond to the location, size, and shape of the mask rather than memorize a marker ID or a fixed trajectory associated with one marker.

\textbf{RQ2: Hybrid local-to-global surface finishing.} Can a hybrid workflow combining force-aware IL-based local processing and geometry-based full-coverage planning provide a practical local-to-global robotic polishing framework? Before standardized physical defects are introduced, the evaluation focuses on ROI coverage, spillover, contact stability, and execution feasibility rather than physical-defect removal performance.

\textbf{RQ3: Rapid response to initial-condition variations.} Can a vision-conditioned IL policy achieve comparable task success with lower and more consistent online computation time than a conventional geometry-based planning pipeline under variations in workpiece pose and robot initial configuration? The comparison is between a classical perception--registration--planning pipeline and a vision-conditioned IL policy, not between ``vision'' and ``learning'' in isolation.

\textbf{RQ4: Contribution of force-aware observation and action.} Do six-axis force/torque observations and learned force references improve contact stability and task execution compared with pose-only IL and fixed-force execution? This question separates the effect of force observation, force action, and admittance-based execution.

\section{Contributions}
The thesis makes the following planned contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item a force-aware imitation-learning framework that uses ROI masks, visual observation, proprioception, and force/torque history to generate local pose--force trajectories;
    \item a defect-map-conditioned local polishing architecture in which ArUco markers are used only as an external command interface for ROI-mask generation and are not provided as policy observations;
    \item a local-to-global hybrid surface-finishing framework that assigns state-dependent local processing to force-aware IL and deterministic full-surface coverage to vision/geometry-based planning;
    \item an experimental framework that compares classical geometry-based planning and IL policy execution for pose-randomized aluminum deburring, separating task success, online latency, cycle time, and contact stability.
\end{enumerate}
These contributions are stated as proposed and evaluated components. Superiority over the baselines is a hypothesis to be tested, not an assumed result.
