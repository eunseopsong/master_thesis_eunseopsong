\section{Background and Motivation}
Robotic polishing is a contact-rich manufacturing process in which the robot must coordinate geometric motion with the physical interaction between a compliant tool and a workpiece. A path that visits the intended surface region is necessary, but it is not sufficient for a consistent finish. The applied normal force, the sliding velocity, and the tool orientation jointly affect contact stability, dwell, and material removal. Errors in any of these variables can cause intermittent contact, excessive loading, nonuniform processing, or surface damage~\cite{preston1927theory,mohsin2019path,min2024force}.

Classical polishing systems address this problem with geometric path planning and force or compliance control. Such systems can be highly repeatable when the workpiece geometry, contact model, fixture, and process parameters are known. Their performance nevertheless depends on accurate modeling and tuning. Local defects, fixture tolerances, uncertain compliance, and operator-specific adjustments are difficult to encode in a fixed analytic trajectory. These limitations motivate learning task-specific contact behavior from human demonstrations.

Imitation learning has become a practical method for acquiring visuomotor skills from demonstrations~\cite{zhao2023act,chi2025diffusionpolicy}. In polishing, however, visual and kinematic observations alone do not uniquely describe the interaction. Similar tool poses can correspond to approach, stable contact, excessive loading, or contact loss. Force/torque sensing supplies direct evidence of this latent contact state. Recent force-aware methods demonstrate the value of predicting or reacting to force during contact-rich manipulation~\cite{zhou2024admitdiff,liu2025forcemimic,he2025foar,tsuji2025contactsurvey}. Their existence also makes the scope of the present thesis precise: the contribution is not force sensing by itself, but its integration into a polishing-oriented demonstration, prediction, and compliant-execution framework.

\section{From Motion Reproduction to Contact Reproduction}
A polishing demonstration contains at least two coupled forms of information. The motion component describes where and how the tool should travel. The interaction component describes the force developed while that motion is executed. Replaying only the demonstrated path assumes that the robot and demonstrator encounter identical geometry and compliance. This assumption is fragile because a small surface-height error can substantially change the contact force without visibly changing the tangential path.

FAIRE therefore treats the desired force trajectory as part of the learned action. The force trajectory is not interpreted as a direct actuator-level command. Instead, a low-level admittance controller converts force-tracking error into a compliant Cartesian correction. This separation assigns complementary responsibilities to learning and control: imitation learning predicts the demonstrated task reference, while admittance control realizes that reference under local contact variation.

Contact is also temporal. An instantaneous wrench cannot always distinguish contact establishment from steady sliding or contact loss. FAIRE uses a gated recurrent unit (GRU) to encode a recent six-axis wrench history. The history representation is fused with visual context and robot state to predict short-horizon pose and force references. During execution, bounded residual reinforcement-learning (RL) policies adjust sliding velocity and tool orientation as precision corrections without replacing the nominal imitation policy.

\section{Research Questions}
The thesis is organized around three research questions inherited from the FAIRE manuscript.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{RQ\arabic*:},leftmargin=2.2cm]
  \item Does predicting a time-varying motion--force trajectory and realizing it through admittance control reproduce demonstrated contact behavior better than pose-only, force-observation-only, fixed-force, phase-force, or one-step-force interfaces?
  \item How should contact history be represented for continuous polishing, and does a separated GRU-based wrench-history encoder improve force-trajectory tracking relative to unified, current-force, nonrecurrent, and LSTM variants?
  \item Do bounded residual policies for sliding velocity and tool-normal alignment improve force tracking, alignment, surface-quality consistency, or task time beyond imitation plus admittance execution?
\end{enumerate}

\section{Contributions}
The contributions are threefold.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A VR-calibrated motion--force teaching system transfers synchronized position, orientation, wrench, and image demonstrations from a handheld device equipped with a six-axis force/torque sensor into robot-executable polishing trajectories.
  \item A GRU-based force-history encoder represents contact evolution within a force-aware imitation policy that predicts reference pose trajectories and desired force trajectories.
  \item A residual RL-augmented admittance execution layer applies bounded sliding-velocity and tool-orientation corrections for precision polishing while retaining the nominal learned motion--force trajectory as the task-level reference.
\end{enumerate}

These contributions are intentionally framed as an integrated precision-polishing system. FAIRE does not claim to introduce force sensing, force prediction, recurrent wrench encoding, admittance control, or residual learning in isolation.

\section{Scope and Thesis Status}
The target task is polishing curved headlight surfaces. Vision is used to provide task-region context; autonomous defect detection or region-of-interest discovery is outside the claimed contribution. The source FAIRE repository is a review-version manuscript draft whose experimental tables, hardware identifiers, network dimensions, controller parameters, and final figures remain incomplete. This thesis preserves that evidentiary boundary. Missing implementation details and measurements are labeled as planned or TBD, and no numerical advantage is asserted before the corresponding experiment is completed.
