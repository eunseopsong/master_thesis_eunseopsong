\chapter{Conclusions and Future Work}
\section{Conclusions}
This thesis draft reformulates the robotic surface-finishing problem as a role-allocation problem between geometric planning and force-aware imitation learning. Geometry-based planning is used where the target surface, tool footprint, and coverage objective can be explicitly specified. ROI-mask-conditioned force-aware IL is used where local behavior depends on the requested region, initial condition, and contact state. A low-level admittance controller provides the shared contact-execution layer.

The proposed framework is evaluated through two planned workpieces. The automotive headlight study tests defect-map- or ROI-mask-conditioned local polishing followed by full-coverage geometric polishing. The ArUco marker is treated only as a temporary command interface for generating an ROI mask and not as a defect-inspection sensor. The aluminum study compares a strong classical perception--registration--planning pipeline with a vision-conditioned IL policy under randomized workpiece pose and robot initial configuration. The key hypothesis is comparable task success with reduced and more consistent online computation time, not task feasibility by IL alone.

The thesis also separates the roles of force observation, learned force reference, and admittance execution. This separation is necessary to show whether force-aware IL improves contact stability beyond pose-only imitation learning and fixed-force execution.

\section{Limitations}
This draft does not yet report real-robot quantitative results. Task success, force RMSE, contact-loss count, latency, cycle time, and surface-quality metrics remain \todoexp{to be measured}. The repository also does not contain the final dataset or controller code needed to verify the exact action channel, so the pose--force interface is written with explicit TODOs where implementation details must be confirmed.

The headlight marker interface does not implement automatic scratch inspection. It only emulates an external defect request. The aluminum benchmark does not establish industrial deburring quality unless actual burr removal, residual burr height, chamfer uniformity, and surface damage are measured. The tool-footprint model is approximate and depends on pad compression, curvature, force, and tool wear. The IL policy can fail outside the demonstrated mask and pose distributions, and force-aware execution still requires conservative safety limits.

\section{Future Work}
Future work includes replacing the ArUco command interface with a real high-resolution defect-inspection system, validating real defect removal and surface restoration, expanding pose and ROI distributions, adding safety-filtered IL or MPC refinement, estimating tool footprint and contact area online, measuring industrial cycle time across larger batches, and integrating material-removal quality feedback. Process-level optimization such as speed, spindle, abrasive, or admittance-gain adaptation should be revisited only after the geometry/IL role allocation and contact-execution layer are validated experimentally.
